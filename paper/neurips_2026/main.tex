\begin{abstract}
Industrial CAD code generation requires the fusion of spatial understanding, parametric reasoning, and program synthesis:
producing executable code that satisfies industry tolerances, feature detail, and the unwritten engineering conventions
that distinguish a manufacturable part from primitive geometry.
Frontier multimodal language models exhibit nascent capability along each axis individually,
yet their joint alignment for industrial CAD remains under-evaluated.
We present \textbf{BenchCAD}, a unified, capability-decomposed evaluation framework whose four task categories
correspond to stages of practising CAD-engineering work:
image-to-code, image- and code-conditioned numeric QA, and instruction-guided editing with verified before/after pairs.
The dataset contains 17{,}900 execution-verified CadQuery parts across 106 industrial part families anchored to
47 ISO/DIN/EN/ASME/IEC specification codes (twist drills, bevel gears, compression springs, propeller, eyebolt),
exercising 45+ distinct CadQuery operations including helical sweeps, lofts, twist-extrudes,
and parametric involute-gear construction.
Score differences between paired image/code tasks isolate which sub-capability
(visual perception, geometric-to-parametric abstraction, or code synthesis) drives a model's remaining gap.
Across 10+ frontier models, we identify three diagnostic phenomena that currently preclude reliable, harnessed CAD design in industry: 
(1) a \emph{Holistic Spatial and Detailing Deficit}, where models fail to reconstruct overall 3D structures and consistently omit fine geometric details; 
(2) an \emph{Industrial Common Sense Gap}, where unwritten engineering conventions are completely absent from the synthesized code; 
and (3) a \emph{CAD Operational Blindspot}, where models overfit to rudimentary sketch-and-extrude data, lacking the domain knowledge to emit essential operations such as helical sweeps and parametric involute-gear construction.
Today's frontier models recover the outer envelope of an industrial part but not the parametric structure underlying it.
Supervised fine-tuning on BenchCAD-Synth raises in-distribution essential-op pass rate
from $\sim$0\% to $\sim$85\% (Figure~\ref{fig:generalisation_gap}),
yet the gap to unseen-family generalisation remains substantial ---
confirming that industrial CAD code generation is a difficult open challenge.

\end{abstract}
