Large multimodal language models now demonstrate three core capabilities, each individually validated on major benchmarks: 3D and spatial reasoning from images and code~\citep{MMSIBench2026}, multi-step CAD operations reasoning~\citep{Wu2021,Khan2024}, and code generation~\citep{Chou2026}.
Programmatic computer-aided design---writing parametric scripts in CadQuery~\citep{CadQuerySoftware} or similar APIs that compile to manufacturable solid geometry---sits at the convergence of all three: producing executable CAD code from an engineering image requires reading geometry, abstracting it into parametric structure, and emitting the precise code.
This convergence makes industrial CAD arguably the next domain where frontier models will land in production. Engineering teams already work in parametric environments, most industrially produced CAD parts are variants of a commonly used set of standard families (gears, fasteners, bearings, springs, brackets, pipes), the dominant workflow involves \emph{editing} an existing parametric program rather than authoring a greenfield design, and the ultimate output is aimed for manufacturable in mass production.
What separates an \emph{industrial} part from a primitive shape is rarely its silhouette---it is the strict compliance with unwritten engineering conventions that every practicing designer treats as default (e.g., chamfered grip edges, closed-and-ground spring end coils, involute gear-tooth profiles, fillets at sharp internal corners). These are conventions that no single end-to-end geometry metric directly rewards.
Several recent works have begun to deliver pieces of this promise~\citep{Khan2024,Guan2025,Rukhovich2025,Alrashedy2025,Kolodiazhnyi2026}, yet no public benchmark currently measures, in a systematic and capability-decomposed way, whether today's frontier models clear the stringent bar this domain demands.

A careful examination of recent benchmarks reveals a concerning pattern: even though models that exhibit the highest performance on IoU and Chamfer distance often generate parts that \emph{appear} close in general geometry but fail to capture critical engineering details—arguably the most valuable aspect of industrial tasks when evaluated against the ground truth. 
\textbf{The geometry passes the eye; it fails the caliper.} This underlying failure mode is structural. 
A model can successfully approximate the outer envelope of an industrial part without ever inferring the underlying parametric logic or corresponding CAD operations, thereby neglecting essential engineering features. 
Consequently, a single metric cannot comprehensively measure or distinguish true model capabilities among various industry examples.

To make this distinction measurable, we propose decomposing the image-to-code pipeline into a four-rung \emph{parametric pyramid} for benchmarking purposes: 
visual and 3D feature recognition at the base, followed sequentially by CAD code and operation understanding, industrial parametric abstraction, and spatial reasoning and code synthesis at the apex. 
While prior CAD code-generation benchmarks focus predominantly on the base, the higher rungs—the critical steps that distinguish a superficial shape match from an industrially viable parametric program—remain largely absent from current public evaluations.

To address this measurement gap, we introduce BenchCAD, a unified, capability-decomposed evaluation framework for large-model CAD capability. BenchCAD contains 17{,}900 verified CadQuery parts across 106 industrial families.
Crucially, hand-crafted by domain experts directly from 106 industry commonly used parts with design and parameter following 47 industrial standards (e.g., ISO/ASME). The dataset exercises 35+ distinct CAD operations (e.g., helical sweeps, lofts) largely absent from prior corpora.

We evaluate models across four tasks: img2cq (image-to-code), vision QA, Code QA, and code edit (instruction-guided editing). All QA pairs and editing instructions are manually annotated by experts to ensure rigorous bottleneck analysis.

Across 10+ frontier models, we identify three primary phenomena precluding industry-grade CAD generation: (1) a Holistic Spatial and Detailing Deficit, capturing global shapes but losing local details; (2) a CAD Operational Blindspot, failing on essential modeling operations; and (3) an Industrial Common Sense and Parametric Abstraction Gap, struggling with unwritten engineering conventions. Finally, our SFT and RL reveal that while models show promising out-of-distribution generalization for CAD operations, closing the residual performance gap remains highly challenging, underscoring the steep difficulty of this domain.

\paragraph{Contributions.} 
(i) The \textbf{BenchCAD dataset and CadGen pipeline}: 17{,}900 verified parametric CAD parts spanning 106 families (following 47 ISO/DIN/EN/ASME/IEC industrial standard), 2{,}400 paired multi-aspect image and code QA questions covering from basic CAD operations and spatial understanding to parametric reasoning, and 748 curated edit pairs. 
All data and code are released under \texttt{BenchCAD/cad\_bench} on Hugging Face under CC-BY-4.0. 
(ii) A \textbf{unified, capability-decomposed evaluation framework} featuring the first publicly released CAD benchmark that combines execution-verified before/after CadQuery edit pairs with a non-target preservation metric. 
(iii) A \textbf{systematic, capability-decomposed evaluation methodology} that breaks the holistic CAD-generation capability into multiple measurable sub-dimensions and quantifies each independently, rather than collapsing it into a single end-to-end score.
Applied to 10+ frontier vision-language and code-language models, this decomposition surfaces three named diagnostic phenomena---Holistic Spatial and Detailing Deficit， Industrial Common Sense Gap, and CAD Operational Blindspot---refocusing the CAD-LM research agenda toward comprehensive 3D understanding, grounding engineering conventions, and expanding operational coverage in training corpora.
