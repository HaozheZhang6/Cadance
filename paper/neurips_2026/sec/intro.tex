What separates an \emph{industrial} part from a primitive shape is rarely its silhouette---it is the strict compliance with unwritten engineering conventions that every practicing designer treats as default (e.g., chamfered grip edges, closed-and-ground spring end coils, involute gear-tooth profiles, fillets at sharp internal corners). These are conventions that no single end-to-end geometry metric directly rewards.
Several recent works have begun to deliver pieces of this promise\hc{this is a bit abstract}~\citep{Khan2024,Guan2025,Rukhovich2025,Alrashedy2025,Kolodiazhnyi2026}, yet no public benchmark currently measures, in a systematic and capability-decomposed way, whether today's frontier models clear the stringent bar this domain demands.

Recent benchmarks reveal a concerning pattern: even though models that exhibit the highest performance on metrics like IoU\hc{citation} and Chamfer distance\hc{citation} often generate parts that \emph{appear} close in general geometry but fail to capture critical engineering details—arguably the most valuable aspect of industrial tasks when evaluated against the ground truth. 
\textbf{The geometry passes the eye; it fails the caliper.} This underlying failure mode is structural. 
A model can successfully approximate the outer envelope of an industrial part without ever inferring the underlying parametric logic or corresponding CAD operations, thereby neglecting essential engineering features. 
Consequently, a single metric cannot comprehensively measure or distinguish true model capabilities among various industry examples.

To make this distinction measurable, we propose decomposing the image-to-code pipeline into a four-rung \emph{parametric pyramid} for benchmarking purposes: 
visual and 3D feature recognition at the base, followed sequentially by CAD code and operation understanding, industrial parametric abstraction, and spatial reasoning and code synthesis at the apex. 
While prior CAD code-generation benchmarks\hc{citations} focus predominantly on the base, the higher rungs—the critical steps that distinguish an apparent shape match from an industrially viable parametric program—remain largely absent from current public evaluations.

To address this measurement gap, we introduce BenchCAD, a unified, capability-decomposed evaluation framework for large-model CAD capability. BenchCAD contains 17{,}900 verified CadQuery parts across 106 industrial families.
Crucially, the parts are hand-crafted by domain experts from commonly used industrial designs, with geometry and parameter relations following 47 industrial standards (e.g., ISO/ASME). The dataset exercises 35+ distinct CAD operations (e.g., helical sweeps, lofts) that are largely absent from prior corpora.

BenchCAD targets three practical CAD-agent workflows: generation, editing, and design QA. We evaluate these through four task families: \texttt{img2cq}, \texttt{edit\_code}, \texttt{qa\_img}, and \texttt{qa\_code}\hc{this is a bit informal. Maybe briefly introduce these four tasks?}, with expert-annotated QA pairs and edit instructions.

Across 10+ frontier models, we identify three primary phenomena precluding industry-grade CAD generation: (1) a Holistic Spatial and Detailing Deficit, capturing global shapes but losing local details; (2) a CAD Operational Blindspot, failing on essential modeling operations; and (3) an Industrial Common Sense and Parametric Abstraction Gap, struggling with unwritten engineering conventions. Finally, our SFT and RL\hc{full name, citations, and briefly introduce how we apply these techniques} reveal that while models show promising out-of-distribution generalization for CAD operations, closing the residual performance gap remains highly challenging, underscoring the steep difficulty of this domain.
